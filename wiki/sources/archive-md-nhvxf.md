# The jobs apocalypse: a (very) short history

**Summary:** 
This article discusses the widespread fear that artificial intelligence will cause unprecedented mass unemployment. While both the public and tech leaders express alarm, economists remain skeptical, pointing out that the labor market is currently strong and historical data shows that technological diffusion is typically slow. Comparing the potential AI disruption to historical shifts, such as the mechanization of agriculture and the Industrial Revolution, the article argues that major technological changes have never resulted in prolonged mass unemployment. Instead, negative impacts on workers historically came more from inflation and political decisions (like tariffs) than from the machines themselves. The author concludes that while significant disruption is possible—which would likely first manifest as a recession with high productivity and profits but stagnant wages—predicting a permanent "jobs apocalypse" ignores historical precedent.

**Key Claims:**
- The current fear of job loss due to AI is higher than during the 2007-09 financial crisis.
- Economists argue against the "lump of labour fallacy", suggesting that technology enriches certain sectors, which creates new jobs.
- Historical technological diffusion (e.g., tractors, steam engines) was slow, allowing the workforce time to adapt.
- The "Engels' pause" during the Industrial Revolution was characterized by slow wage growth but also rapid population growth; workers' struggles were exacerbated by high food costs and tariffs rather than just automation.
- Any future AI disruption will likely present as an economic recession, cleansing unproductive jobs, which is a pattern seen in past economic downturns.
